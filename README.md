## Background
Connor McDavid, number one pick in the 2015 NHL Draft, captain of the Edmonton Oilers, widely considered by many, if not all, to be the greatest hockey player in the modern era of the NHL always seems to come up short on the biggest stages in hockey. McDavid's greatness is undeniable. A 3 time Hart Memorial Trophy (MVP), 4 time Ted Lindsay (Players' MVP), 5 time Art Ross Trophy (Scoring Leader), Maurice "Rocket" Richard Trophy (Top Goal Scorer), Conn Smythe Trophy (Playoff MVP) recipient, member of the NHL All-Rookie Team, and 5 time member of the NHL First All-Star Team, the resume speaks for itself. 

However there are two glaring accolades that are missing from McDavid's Hall of Fame worthy resume: Lord Stanley's Cup and Olympic Gold. In 2024, McDavid  led the Oilers back to a Stanley Cup Finals appearance after 18 years, but would lose in a heartbreaking 7 game fashion to the Florida Panthers. In 2025, McDavid would receive a chance at redemption as the Oilers would once again face the Panthers for the Stanley Cup, but in seeming regression, the Oilers would instead lose in 6 games to the Panthers. Then, in 2024-25 the NHL would introduce the inaugural Four Nations Faceoff in which NHL players would have the chance to represent their country. McDavid would come in to the tournament backed with an incomprehensibly loaded Team Canada consisting of the likes of star Colorado Avalanche Center Nathan MacKinnon, standout defenseman Cale Makar, and all time great Sidney Crosby of the Pittsburgh Penguins, just to name a few. McDavid and Team Canada would actually end up winning the tournament, defeating the gritty United States in an overtime win that was ended with a game winning goal by none other than McDavid himself. But then the scene would shift in the 2025-26 season, with the arrival of the Milan Cortina Winter Olympic Games. McDavid would arrive in Milan backed with essentially the same roster from the Four Nations Faceoff, a roster that wasn't subtracted from but added to. In what seems like a curse, McDavid and the star studded Team Canada would fall to the United States in overtime. 

This is just another tale of a superstar who just cannot seem to win the big games like a Josh Allen, Lamar Jackson, or Paul George. But McDavid's different. He's not just a superstar, but a once in a lifetime generational talent who has come so close on numerous occasions but has fallen just short so many times. This project attempts to quantify the reason why Connor McDavid, a consistent performer in the regular season and early rounds of the playoffs, continues to lose in the biggest stages of hockey.

## Research Questions
1. Does McDavid's individual performance measurably decline in 
   elimination and championship-round games?
2. Does Edmonton's team construction fail around him in big games?
3. Do elite defensive systems and goaltending specifically 
   suppress him in ways that don't appear in earlier rounds?
